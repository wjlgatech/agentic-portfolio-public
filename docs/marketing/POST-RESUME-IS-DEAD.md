# The Résumé Is Dead. Long Live the Living Portfolio.

*Why an agentic portfolio is how you thrive in the AI age — not the thing you lose your job to.*
*(LinkedIn long-form. Companion to `OUTREACH.md` — the wedge post's big sibling. Every product claim below is live; every citation is real.)*

---

We need to talk about your résumé.

It's a PDF. It's two pages. It says "results-driven professional" — which, statistically, is what everyone else's says too. It sits in a folder called `Resume_FINAL_v7_ACTUAL_FINAL.pdf`, and its entire career strategy is to be skimmed by a stranger for **7.4 seconds** — that's the average initial screen recruiters gave a résumé in The Ladders' eye-tracking study.¹ Seven-point-four seconds. You have spent more time choosing a font.

And that's when a *human* reads it. Mostly, one doesn't. Harvard Business School's "Hidden Workers" research found that more than 90% of employers filter applications through automated systems first — and **88% of employers admit those systems screen out qualified candidates** because their PDF didn't recite the right keywords.² Your résumé isn't being rejected. It's being *unread* by a robot with a checklist.

So here's the uncomfortable arithmetic of the AI age: machines are already deciding whether you get seen, and your side of that conversation is… a static document. A dead one. It can't answer a question. It can't show a receipt. It can't say "here's the repo, here's the video, here's the proof." It just lies there, results-drivenly.

The résumé is dead. It just hasn't told HR yet.

## The eleven-year-old who out-hired all of us

Let me tell you about an eleven-year-old. Call him Leo.

Leo walks dogs. Six dogs, four neighbors, one very suspicious cat he refuses to discuss. Last month, Leo's mom helped him spend ten minutes making something most adults don't have: a living portfolio with its own AI agent.

Not a flyer. Not a "résumé" (what would it say — *Objective: dogs*?). A little live website where a neighbor can type: *"Can he handle a big dog?"* — and Leo's agent answers, grounded in what's actually there: photos from ninety walks, a note from Mrs. Alvarez about her anxious German Shepherd, his rain-or-shine streak. Ask it something it can't back up — *"is he certified in canine first aid?"* — and it says, honestly: **unverified**. Leo's agent doesn't exaggerate. Leo's agent has more professional integrity than several LinkedIn influencers I could name.

Here's the part that got me. A new family on the street asked Leo why he made it. He said:

*"So people who need help can find out if I can actually help them. Even when I'm at school."*

That's it. That's the whole philosophy, out of the mouth of a sixth-grader: **proof of service, available 24/7, honest about its limits.** Leo isn't optimizing keywords. Leo is *showing his work* — and letting anyone, human or AI, check it.

An eleven-year-old can build a truthful, always-on ambassador for what he does for other people.

So — respectfully — why is your career still represented by a PDF that plays dead?

## This isn't a vibe. The research has been saying it for 50 years.

Economists have known since Michael Spence's Nobel-winning 1973 work that hiring runs on **signals** — credible, costly-to-fake evidence of ability.³ A résumé was always just a signal container. And it's a leaky one: cheap to inflate, impossible to interrogate.

Meanwhile, researchers studying how developers actually get hired found that recruiters increasingly bypass the PDF entirely and read **activity traces** — public work, commit histories, real artifacts — because visible work is a far more reliable signal than self-description.⁴ "Show, don't tell" isn't writing advice anymore. It's labor economics.

Now add the AI wave. The "GPTs are GPTs" study estimates that around **80% of U.S. workers** have at least 10% of their tasks exposed to large language models — about one in five workers, more than half.⁵ Scary headline, but here's the half nobody quotes: David Autor's team traced job titles across eight decades and found that **roughly 60% of the jobs people held in 2018 didn't exist in 1940**.⁶ Technology doesn't just delete work — it mints new work, and the people who catch the new work first are the ones *visibly making things* when it arrives.

And if you want to know why Leo learned more in ten minutes of making than in a semester of worksheets — Seymour Papert wrote the book on it in 1980: people learn most powerfully by **constructing things that matter to others**, not by consuming content.⁷ Making your portfolio isn't marketing homework. It's the learning itself.

Put the four together and the conclusion writes itself:

> Signals beat claims. Traces beat signals. AI reads everything, 24/7.
> So the winning move is a **living, queryable, honest body of proof** — not a better-formatted obituary.

## What you actually get (the "very rewarding" part)

- **An ambassador that never sleeps.** Recruiters, clients, collaborators — and increasingly *their* AI agents — can ask your portfolio questions at 3am and get grounded answers. Your PDF can't talk to a recruiting bot. Your portfolio literally speaks agent-to-agent (it publishes a standard agent card other AIs can discover and query).
- **Receipts, not adjectives.** It audits claims against your live public GitHub and shows verdicts — corroborated, partial, unverified. Honesty is the differentiator; in a world drowning in AI-generated fluff, *provable* is the new premium.
- **You own it.** Your page, your owner key, your leads (when your agent captures interest from a visitor, only *you* see that pipeline). No contact harvesting, ever — the network grows only by people proudly sharing their own work.
- **It stays alive.** One click (or a daily schedule) pulls in your newest repos and videos. A living portfolio, unlike a certain PDF, does not require exhumation before each job application.
- **You shape what it becomes.** Tell the agent a feature idea or a complaint, in plain words. It goes into a weekly build review; when your suggestion ships, you get a note — and your portfolio already runs the update. You're not a user. You're a co-builder.
